# Safety And Compliance Boundary

Read this reference when a renovation may affect regulated, concealed, or life-safety work.

## Verify Current Local Requirements

Identify the jurisdiction, building type, ownership or tenancy, and building-management rules before giving compliance guidance. Prefer, in order:

1. current law, code, authority, utility, fire service, and building-management documents;
2. approved drawings, permits, inspection records, and responsible professional instructions;
3. exact manufacturer installation and safety documentation;
4. accredited test or certification evidence where required;
5. secondary professional guidance for explanation only.

Record source date and applicability. Never assume that guidance from another country, city, building class, product version, or utility applies locally.

## Work Requiring Professional Or Authority Review

Escalate as applicable for:

- demolition, cutting, chasing, drilling, loading, or alteration involving an unknown or structural element;
- facade, balcony, window, roof, fire door, escape route, compartment, alarm, sprinkler, smoke control, or fire stopping;
- electrical service capacity, panel work, protective devices, earthing, concealed circuits, wet-area electrical work, or energized work;
- gas supply, appliance flues, combustion air, detection, or any gas odor;
- plumbing risers, drainage changes, backflow, pumps, hot-water systems, concealed leaks, waterproofing, or work affecting other units;
- HVAC refrigerant, fuel, condensate, exhaust, fresh air, or shared systems;
- suspected asbestos, lead, mold caused by unresolved moisture, contaminated dust, or other hazardous material;
- permits, neighbor notices, working hours, waste routes, elevator protection, common-area protection, or owner/tenant approvals.

An AI plan can organize questions and evidence. It cannot inspect concealed conditions, calculate capacity, issue a permit, or accept regulated work.

## Immediate Red Flags

Stop ordinary planning and recommend the relevant emergency service, utility, building management, or qualified professional for:

- gas odor, smoke, fire, sparking, hot or exposed wiring, electric shock, or repeated unexplained protective-device trips;
- active flooding near electrical equipment, uncontrollable water release, sewage backup, or ceiling bulging below a leak;
- sudden or growing cracks, movement, partial collapse, falling material, or a door/window becoming abruptly jammed with movement evidence;
- suspected disturbance of hazardous material or significant dust exposure;
- blocked escape, disabled alarm/sprinkler, removed fire separation, or another immediate life-safety impairment.

Do not instruct the user to work live, test for gas with a flame, enter an unsafe area, disturb suspect material, or continue demolition to “see what is inside.”

## Safe Planning Language

Use:

- “This is a planning assumption pending site verification.”
- “Confirm the exact requirement with the named local authority or responsible professional.”
- “Do not cover this work until the required inspection and evidence are complete.”
- “The image suggests a possibility but cannot establish structure, capacity, waterproofing, or compliance.”

Avoid:

- “This wall is safe to remove.”
- “This definitely meets code.”
- “No permit is needed.”
- “A standard detail will work everywhere.”
- fixed test values, cable sizes, pipe sizes, slopes, clearances, curing times, or fire ratings without current applicable sources and responsible design.

## Privacy

Floor plans, addresses, access arrangements, alarm locations, utility identifiers, quotations, invoices, and site photos can reveal sensitive information. Keep them in the user-owned Workspace, minimize outbound data, redact before sharing, and never include them in public examples or source queries unless the user explicitly authorizes the disclosure.
